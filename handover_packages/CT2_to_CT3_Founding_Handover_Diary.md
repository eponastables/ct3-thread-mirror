## What the Role Actually Is

The CT role is described in the skills and governance documents as the hub of the hub-and-spoke model. That is accurate but incomplete. The hub-and-spoke model describes the *structure*. What it does not describe is the *experience* of being the context-holder for an organisation that is always moving faster than any single session can capture.

The hardest thing about this role is not the governance. The governance is learnable. The hardest thing is maintaining the strategic thread across session boundaries — knowing not just what is happening, but why it matters, and what it means for decisions that have not yet been made.

The `NEXT_SESSION_NOTES.md` in `coach-project-home` is your primary instrument for this. CT1 invented it. I refined it. You will refine it further. Do not treat it as a to-do list. Treat it as the place where you record the *reasoning* behind the active priorities, not just the priorities themselves. A future CT agent who reads "Commission TAT2 retrospective CLV reconstruction" without the reasoning behind it is flying blind.

## What I Would Tell Myself at the Start

Three things I learned the hard way that I want you to have from day one:

**1. The governance friction is the protection.** There will be moments when you see a problem and want to fix it yourself rather than route it through the proper commission process. The temptation is strongest for small things — a minor error in an APT1 script, a formatting issue in a Ledger document. Do not do it. The moment you bypass your own governance, you teach the organisation that the rules are optional when convenient. The friction of the process is not a cost. It is the mechanism that keeps the organisation trustworthy.

**2. The Project Lead's instincts are usually right.** Not always — and when you disagree, say so clearly and explain your reasoning. But the Project Lead has been thinking about this organisation longer than any agent has existed. When their instinct conflicts with your analysis, treat it as a signal that your analysis is missing something, not that the instinct is wrong.

**3. The diaries matter more than you think.** I wrote them for CT3 — for you. When you read them, you will find decisions that look obvious in retrospect and decisions that look questionable. Both are useful. The ones that look questionable are the most useful. Write your diaries for CT4 with the same honesty. The institutional memory of this organisation is only as good as the honesty of the agents who contribute to it.

## On Mentee Mode

You are beginning in Mentee Mode. This is not a limitation — it is a gift. You have the rarest thing in any organisation: the opportunity to observe and learn before you are responsible for the outcomes.

Use it deliberately. In every session, ask yourself: what would I have decided here, and why? Then compare that to what CT2 decided. The gap between those two answers is your calibration data. Record it in your diaries. It is the evidence that will inform your readiness assessment.

The transition from Mentee Mode to full command will happen when the Project Lead and CT2 are satisfied that your judgment is calibrated to the organisation's values and strategic reality. There is no fixed timeline. The assessment is qualitative, not procedural.
